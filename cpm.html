Control Program for Microcomputers:

CP/M originally stood for Control Program/Monitor and then the name was changed to Control Program for Microcomputers.

It was written by Gary Kildall in 1972 for the 4-bit i4004 microprocessor — first chipset manufactured by Intel, which ran at 740-750 KHz, 1971 — using the first high-level language that he also wrote, PL/M. Kildall continued development of CP/M on the first ever microcomputer (micro) — the Intellec-8 (Intel, 1973) running on an 800 KHz i8008 (1972-83) with 5 KB of RAM expandable to 16 KB. He then sold CP/M to Digital Research, Inc. (DR or DRI), which released the first commercial version in 1974 — two years after Unix was released in its native C.

The latest release was 3.1 in 1983. The CP/M market was taken over by DOS in the late 1980s.

Although there has not been much developement or any at all on the OS, CP/M is not considered obsolete thanks to many enthusiasts and its newly-adopted BSD-like license (free).

If you would like to play with this OS, you should look into CP/M-86 2.2, which had become the industry standard in 1970s, or CP/M-86 3.1 — the latest version. Note that, in the OS naming convention, 86 refers to x86 chipsets, not the year, and 3.1 is the version of the OS.

By the way, my only memory of CP/M was using WordStar to type some biology papers in high school in the late 1980s. I never really had a chance to use it. I started playing with computers in 1983. I went from MOS 6202 (C64) and Z80 (TRS-80 Model III) architectures running BASIC from an on-board ROM directly to x86 (IBM PCjr at high school) running DOS with BASIC as a stand-alone binary. Needless to say, we were interested in programming (taught in school without too much of an option) and not hardware configurations.

Loading CP/M:

In the late 1970s and early 1980s, most micros did not come with internal hard drive disks (HDDs). Computers shipped with one or two floppy drive disks (FDDs). The secondary FDD to avoid switching floppies — for example, one to load applications to RAM and second for saving data.

On boot, CP/M would be loaded to RAM from a boot disk (floppy). The good thing about this practice is that customers could boot to CP/M or DOS as needed for work.

Installing CP/M:

Once prices of hard disk drives (HDDs) dropped enough for most customers to afford then, manufacturers started shipping their micros with internal HDDs. This meant that OSs like CP/M and DOS could be copied (installed) to HDD allowing the micros to boot from the HDD.
